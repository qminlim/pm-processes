🏗️ Agile Foundation

Core concepts, the Manifesto, principles, and mindset.

🌱
What is Agile?
Foundation
▼
  • Iterative development methodology — an umbrella term
  • Originally for software, but applicable to all project types
  • Scrum is most common; others include XP, Lean, Kanban
  • Builds in increments (not as a whole)
  • Planning throughout (not all at once)
  • Delivers products over time — customer sees value faster
  • Welcomes changes vs. discouraging them
📝 Exam Tip

Agile fixes Time & Cost but varies Scope. Traditional fixes Scope but varies Time & Cost. This "inverted triangle" is a common exam question.

📜
Agile Manifesto — 4 Values
Foundation
▼

Created in 2001. "We value the items on the left more, while there is still value in items on the right."

Individuals & InteractionsOVERProcesses & Tools
Working SoftwareOVERComprehensive Documentation
Customer CollaborationOVERContract Negotiation
Responding to ChangeOVERFollowing a Plan
📝 Exam Tip

The right side is NOT worthless — it still has value. Agile simply values the left side more. Don't choose answers that say documentation has zero value.

🔢
12 Guiding Principles
Foundation
▼
  1. Highest priority: satisfy customer via early & continuous valuable delivery
  2. Welcome changing requirements, even late — harness for competitive advantage
  3. Deliver working software frequently (weeks to months; prefer shorter)
  4. Business & developers must work together daily
  5. Build around motivated individuals; give environment, support, and trust
  6. Face-to-face conversation is most efficient & effective method
  7. Working software is the primary measure of progress
  8. Agile processes promote sustainable development — constant pace indefinitely
  9. Continuous attention to technical excellence & good design enhances agility
  10. Simplicity — maximize the amount of work not done
  11. Best architectures emerge from self-organizing teams
  12. Team reflects regularly and adjusts behavior accordingly
📝 Exam Tip

Principle 7: Working software = measure of progress (NOT documentation). Principle 10: Do LESS work, not more — simplicity is an art.

🧠
Agile Mindset
Foundation
▼
  • Welcoming change
  • Working in small value increments
  • Using build-and-feedback loops
  • Learning through discovery
  • Value-driven development
  • Failing fast with learning
  • Continuous delivery
  • Continuous improvement
🤝
Declaration of Interdependence (DOI)
Foundation
▼

Agile and adaptive approaches for linking people, projects, and value (2005).

  • Increase ROI → continuous flow of value
  • Deliver reliable results → customer interactions & shared ownership
  • Expect uncertainty → manage through iterations, anticipation, adaptation
  • Unleash creativity → individuals are the ultimate source of value
  • Boost performance → group accountability & shared responsibility
  • Improve effectiveness → situationally specific strategies & practices
📊
Agile Concurrent Development
Foundation
▼
  • Fund incrementally — opt to extend, redirect, or cancel at a granular level
  • Deliver & realize value steadily
  • Validate designs with users & customers
  • Continuously adapt to risk and change
  • Integrate early & often

🔄 Scrum Framework

Roles, events, artifacts, and the Scrum process in detail.

🏛️
Scrum Pillars
Scrum
▼
TransparencyVisibility to those responsible for the outcome
InspectionTimely checks on how well the project is progressing; looks for problematic deviations
AdaptationAdjusting a process to minimize further issues when an inspection shows a problem
📝 Exam Tip

Scrum is built on TIA — Transparency, Inspection, Adaptation. These 3 pillars underpin every Scrum activity.

👤
Scrum Roles
Scrum
▼
Product Owner Owns product vision; defines features & release dates; prioritizes by market value; can change priorities every sprint
ScrumMaster Facilitates process; protects team from external interruption; enhances productivity; assists Product Owner
Development Team Small, cross-functional, self-organizing; focuses on steady delivery; manages own work within sprints
📅
Sprint Planning Meeting
Scrum
▼
  • Determines what work will be done in the sprint
  • Team predicts deliverables based on estimates, capacity, past performance
  • Team determines how functionality will be built
  • Output: Sprint Backlog
⏱️
Sprints (Iterations)
Scrum
▼
  • Timeboxed iteration of 1–4 weeks
  • Produces a potentially releasable product increment
  • No changes during sprint that would affect sprint goal
  • Development team composition stays the same throughout
📝 Exam Tip

Sprint = Iteration. Typical sprint is 1–4 weeks. No mid-sprint changes to sprint goal — changes go to the next sprint backlog.

☀️
Daily Scrum (Standup)
Scrum
▼
  • 15-minute time-boxed activity
  • Same time and place each day
  • Each member answers 3 questions:
  1. What did I do yesterday?
  2. What will I do today?
  3. Are there any impediments in my way?
🎯
Sprint Review
Scrum
▼
  • Takes place at the end of the sprint
  • Gathers feedback from stakeholders on completed work
  • Team demonstrates completed work
  • Creates a conversation between team & stakeholders
  • Time-boxed: max 1 hour per week of sprint
🔁
Sprint Retrospective
Scrum
▼

Inspect and create a plan for improvements in the next sprint.

  • What went well?
  • What went wrong?
  • What to do more of?
  • What to do less of?
📦
Scrum Artifacts
Scrum
▼
Product Backlog Prioritized, dynamic list of all work. Managed by Product Owner. Sorted by value — most valuable first. Continuously "groomed."
Sprint Backlog Items selected from Product Backlog for the sprint. Only updated by development team. Highly visible view of work.
Product Increment Shippable part of product completed after each sprint. Product Owner & team must agree on "Definition of Done."
✅
Definition of Done (DoD)
Scrum
▼
  • Shared understanding of what "done" means
  • Defined at the beginning of the project
  • Applies globally to the project
  • May include: unit tests, documentation, code writing standards
📝 Exam Tip

DoD ≠ Acceptance Criteria. DoD is a shared team standard; Acceptance Criteria is specific to each user story.

⚡ Extreme Programming (XP)

Software-centric Agile method focused on engineering best practices.

❤️
XP Core Values
XP
▼
SimplicityReduce complexity & waste. "Find the simplest thing that could possibly work."
CommunicationTeam knows what's expected; daily standup is key component
FeedbackGet impressions of correctness early; failing fast allows faster improvement
CourageAllow work to be entirely visible to others
RespectEveryone is accountable; recognize different work styles
📝 Mnemonic

S-C-F-C-R → Simplicity, Communication, Feedback, Courage, Respect

👥
XP Roles
XP
▼
CoachMentor & facilitator; guides the process, helps team stay on track (= ScrumMaster)
CustomerBusiness representative providing requirements & priorities (= Product Owner)
ProgrammersDevelopers who build the product and write the code
TestersHelp customer define and write acceptance tests for user stories
🎮
XP Planning Practices
XP
▼
Release Planning Customer outlines functionality; developers estimate difficulty; push new functionality to production users
Iteration Planning Short development cycles within a release (= Sprints). Conducted at start of every iteration. Customer explains desired functionality; team breaks into tasks.
🧪
XP Engineering Practices
XP
▼
TDDWrite tests before writing code. Code that passes test = correct code.
Pair ProgrammingTwo devs, one keyboard. Real-time code review; spreads knowledge through team.
Continuous IntegrationMerge and test frequently. Brings problems to surface before more code is built on faulty designs.
Collective OwnershipAny pair can improve any code. Higher quality, less risk if someone leaves.
RefactoringRemove redundancy, eliminate unused functionality, rejuvenate obsolete designs. Saves time, increases quality.
Simple DesignCode is testable, browsable, understandable. Do simplest thing that works. Self-organizing teams produce best designs.
Small ReleasesFrequent small releases; demonstrate progress; maintain quality via rigorous testing.
Customer TestsCustomer describes tests; team builds automated tests to prove software works.
Sustainable PaceRepeated overtime is unsustainable. Steady pace optimizes long-term value delivery.
MetaphorUse comparisons that all stakeholders understand to explain how the system works.
Code StandardsConsistent coding standard — code looks as if written by a single knowledgeable programmer.
📝 Exam Tip

TDD writes tests FIRST, then code. This is opposite to traditional development. If code initially fails the test — that's expected and correct behavior.

♻️ Lean & Kanban

Lean software development principles and the Kanban flow system.

🏭
Lean 7 Principles
Lean
▼
Eliminate WasteMinimize partially done work, delays, handoffs, unnecessary features
Empower the TeamRespect team's superior technical knowledge; avoid micro-management
Deliver FastQuickly deliver valuable software and iterate through designs
Optimize the WholeSee the system as more than the sum of its parts
Build Quality InContinuously assure quality throughout development
Defer DecisionsCommit as late as possible — "last responsible moment"
Amplify LearningFacilitate communication early/often; get feedback ASAP; build on what's learned
📝 Mnemonic

Lean came from Toyota's manufacturing system. Principles include visual management tools, customer-defined value, and continuous improvement.

🗑️
7 Wastes of Lean
Lean
▼
  1. Partially done work
  2. Extra Processes
  3. Extra Features
  4. Task Switching
  5. Waiting
  6. Motion
  7. Defects
📝 Mnemonic

PEPTW MD → Partially done, Extra processes, Extra features, Task switching, Waiting, Motion, Defects

📋
Kanban — 5 Core Principles
Kanban
▼

"Kanban" is Japanese for "signboard." Derived from Toyota's lean production system.

Visualize WorkflowSoftware is intangible — make it visible (Kanban board)
Limit WIPLow WIP = increased visibility of issues and bottlenecks
Manage FlowTrack flow through system; identify issues; measure changes
Explicit PoliciesClearly explain how things work for open improvement discussions
Improve CollaborationCollectively own and improve processes through measurement and experimentation
🚦
Kanban Board & WIP Limits
Kanban
▼

An "information radiator" — ensures efficient diffusion of information. Typical columns:

ITEMS
IN PROGRESS
6 card limit
TESTING
4 card limit
DONE
  • WIP = work started but not completed → money spent with no return yet
  • Excessive WIP hides bottlenecks and masks efficiency issues
  • Represents risk in the form of potential rework
📈
Cumulative Flow Diagrams (CFD)
Kanban
▼
  • Stack graphs showing how work progresses through stages
  • Stages tracked: Ready → Dev → Test → Deployed
  • Widening area in a lane = bottleneck at that activity
  • Theory of Constraints: identify and address the bottleneck
📝 Exam Tip

A widening band on a CFD indicates work is accumulating at that stage — it's a bottleneck. The solution is to address that specific constraint.

📅 Adaptive Planning

Estimation, user stories, iteration types, and release planning.

📝
User Stories
Planning
▼

Business functionality involving 1–3 days of work. Acts as agreement between customers and development team.

Format As a <user type> I want to <goal> so that <value>

Example: "As a payroll clerk, I want to view a payroll tax report, so that I can pay them on time."

📝 Mnemonic — 3 C's

Card (written on index card) → Conversation (no details, used to discuss) → Confirmation (acceptance criteria)

💎
INVEST Criteria for User Stories
Planning
▼
I
Independent
Can be reprioritized independently
N
Negotiable
Allows trade-offs based on cost & function
V
Valuable
Clearly states business value
E
Estimatable
Team can estimate how long to complete
S
Small
Between 4–40 hours of work
T
Testable
Can be tested to ensure acceptance
🃏
Estimation Techniques
Planning
▼
Story PointsRelative sizing; all-inclusive (complexity + effort + risk). Use Fibonacci: 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21
Planning PokerWideband Delphi variant. Cards with Fibonacci; fast and collaborative. Prevents groupthink & HIPPO effect.
Affinity EstimatingGroup estimates into categories or collections
T-Shirt SizingPlace stories in t-shirt sizes (XS, S, M, L, XL)
Wideband DelphiAnonymous panel of experts. Prevents bandwagon effect, HIPPO decisions, and groupthink
Ideal TimeTime to complete assuming zero interruptions or unplanned problems
📝 Exam Tip

HIPPO = Highest-Paid Person's Opinion. Planning Poker and Wideband Delphi prevent this by using anonymous estimation.

⚡
Velocity & Cycle Time Formulas
Planning
▼
Iterations Total Points ÷ Avg Velocity = Iterations Remaining

Example: 250 points ÷ 18 points/iteration ≈ 14 more iterations

Cycle Time WIP ÷ Throughput = Cycle Time

Example: 60 pts ÷ 5 pts/day = 12 days

Risk EMV Impact ($) × Probability (%) = EMV
📝 Exam Tip

Throughput = amount of work completed in a given time period. Long cycle times lead to increased WIP, which hides bottlenecks.

🗓️
Types of Iterations
Planning
▼
Iteration 0Sets the stage for development — doesn't build anything; environment setup, architecture decisions
Dev IterationsBuild the product increment — the main sprints
Iteration HHardening sprint at the end — code cleanup, documentation, release preparation
SpikesArchitectural spike: proof of concept. Risk-based spike: investigate to reduce/eliminate risk
🎯
Prioritization Techniques
Planning
▼
MoSCoWMust have · Should have · Could have · Would like (not this time)
Dot VotingEach person gets a set number of dots to distribute to requirements
Monopoly MoneyEach person gets equal funds to distribute to features they value most
100-Point MethodEach person gets 100 points to distribute to individual requirements
Kano AnalysisDelighters/Exciters · Satisfiers · Dissatisfiers · Indifferent
Simple SchemePriority 1, 2, 3 — can be problematic when too many become Priority 1
📝 Exam Tip

MoSCoW is the most commonly tested prioritization technique. "W" stands for "Would like but not this time" — it doesn't mean never.

⏰
Timeboxing
Planning
▼

Fixed-duration periods where activities are undertaken. Unfinished work moves to next timebox.

Daily Standup15 minutes
Retrospectives2 hours
Sprints1–4 weeks
📝 Exam Tip — Parkinson's Law

"Work tends to expand to fill the time given." Timeboxing prevents this by imposing hard limits.

🌀
Progressive Elaboration
Planning
▼
  • Adding more detail as information emerges
  • Applies to: plans, estimates, designs, test scenarios
  • Rolling wave planning: planning at multiple points in time as data becomes available
  • Keep requirements "coarse" then progressively refine them
  • Delays decisions until the "last responsible moment"

Tuckman's 5 Stages of Team Development
Team
▼
1
Forming → Directing
Team comes together, gets to know each other. Little conflict or communication.
2
Storming → Coaching
Team members start having conflicts. Most conflicts take place here.
3
Norming → Supporting
Team begins to agree on best methods. Coming to consensus.
4
Performing → Delegating
Team is performing well, working without conflict.
5
Adjourning
Project completed and team is reassigned.
📝 Exam Tip — Adaptive Leadership

Leadership style adapts to team stage: Form→Direct, Storm→Coach, Norm→Support, Perform→Delegate

Leadership
▼

Leader provides what the team needs to succeed:

  1. Shield team from interruptions
  2. Remove impediments to progress
  3. (Re)Communicate project vision

ManagementMechanical focus → Task/things, Control, Command
LeadershipHumanistic focus → People, Empowerment, Communication

Tracking
▼

💎 Value Delivery & Risk

Value-driven delivery, financial metrics, risk management, and contracting.

💰
Financial Value Metrics
Value
▼
ROIReturn on Investment — ratio of benefits to money invested (usually a %)
IRRInternal Rate of Return — interest rate needed in today's money to receive future amount
NPV/PVNet Present Value — value of future money in today's terms
EVMEarned Value Management — formulas that monitor the value of the project as it progresses
📝 Exam Tip

Higher NPV = better project. Positive NPV means the investment is profitable in today's terms. IRR > cost of capital = good investment.

Value
▼
  • Rate of progress — how many points completed
  • Remaining work — how much is left in the backlog

  • Likely cost remaining
Lagging MetricsInformation about something that has already happened
Leading MetricsInformation about what is or is about to occur

Risk Management
Risk
▼

Risk is considered anti-value — it has potential to remove, erode, or reduce value.

Risk EMV Impact ($) × Probability (%) = Expected Monetary Value
Severity Risk Probability × Risk Impact (scale 1–5)
Risk-Adjusted BacklogAdjusting the backlog after risk response planning
Risk Burndown ChartTracks how risk is being eliminated over time during the project

Agile Contracting
Value
▼

Agile fixes resources and time (cost) and varies functionality.

Graduated Fixed Price Buyer/Seller share risks and rewards. Different hourly rates: finish early / on time / late
Fixed Price Packages Mitigate risks of under/over estimating with fixed work packages
Money for Nothing Customer can cancel contract but compensate for lost opportunity
Change for Free Customer can change requirements as long as scope remains constant
📦
Minimal Viable Product (MVP)
Value
▼
  • Set of functionality complete enough to be useful
  • Small enough not to be an entire project
  • Usually a module in a software system
  • Leads to more opportunity for incremental funding
  • Enables faster feedback and course correction
💳
Technical Debt
Risk
▼
  • Backlog of work caused by not doing regular cleanup
  • If left unaddressed → increases cost of development
  • Makes it harder to implement future changes
  • Refactoring is the solution
📝 Exam Tip

Technical debt is like financial debt — interest accumulates. The longer you ignore it, the more expensive it becomes to fix.

Retrospectives, Kaizen, process analysis, and problem-solving strategies.

🔄
Kaizen & PDCA Cycle
Improvement
▼

Kaizen = Japanese word for continuous improvement. Focus on small, incremental improvements.

🟦
PLAN
🟩
DO
🟨
CHECK
🟥
ACT

PDCA Cycle by Edwards Deming. Maps to Agile's: Plan → Develop → Evaluate → Learn loop.

🪞
Retrospective — 5 Stages
Improvement
▼

Special meeting after each iteration. ~2 hours total. Inspect and improve methods and teamwork.

1
Set the Stage 6 min
Help people focus; encourage participation; outline approach. Activities: Check-In, ESVP (Explorer/Shopper/Vacationer/Prisoner)
2
Gather Data 40 min
Create picture of what happened. Timeline, Triple Nickels (5 groups × 5 min × 5 ideas), Mad/Sad/Glad
3
Generate Insights 25 min
Analyze data. Brainstorming, Five Whys, Fishbone Analysis, Dot Voting
4
Decide What to Do 20 min
How to improve next iteration. Short Subjects, SMART Goals
5
Close Retrospective 20 min
Reflect on retro itself. Plus/Delta: do more of / do less of
📝 Exam Tip

SMART Goals: Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant, Timely. Set these in Stage 4 of the retrospective.

🌊
Value Stream Mapping
Improvement
▼

Optimize the flow of information or materials to complete a process. Reduce waste and unnecessary work.

  1. Identify the product or service
  2. Create a current-state value stream map
  3. Review to find waste
  4. Create a future-state map with desired improvements
  5. Develop a roadmap to implement fixes
  6. Plan to revisit it again (continuous loop)
🦴
Fishbone (Ishikawa) Analysis
Improvement
▼
  • Root cause analysis tool
  • Used in Retrospective Stage 3 (Generate Insights)
  • Diagram shows potential causes of a problem branching from main "spine"
  • Categories typically: People, Process, Equipment, Environment, Materials, Methods
📝 Paired with

Five Whys: Ask "why?" five times to drill down to the root cause of any problem. Both tools support generating insights in retrospectives.

💀
Pre-Mortems
Improvement
▼

Team meeting to identify possible failures before they happen.

  1. Think about what the failures might be
  2. Create a list of reasons that can cause those failures
  3. Review the project plan to determine what can reduce or remove the risks
📝 Exam Tip

Pre-Mortem = proactive risk identification. Retrospective = reactive lessons learned. Don't confuse the two.

🔧
Process Tailoring
Improvement
▼
  • Amend methodology to better fit the project environment
  • Change things for good reason — not just for the sake of change
  • Develop a hybrid approach if needed
  • Process analysis: review and diagnose issues; look for tailoring possibilities
🏆
Lead Time vs. Cycle Time
Improvement
▼
Lead TimeHow long something takes to go through the entire process end-to-end
Cycle TimeHow long something takes to go through a part of the process. Part of lead time.
Cycle Time WIP ÷ Throughput
📝 Exam Tip

Lead Time ≥ Cycle Time always. Cycle time is a subset of lead time. Reducing WIP directly reduces cycle time.
